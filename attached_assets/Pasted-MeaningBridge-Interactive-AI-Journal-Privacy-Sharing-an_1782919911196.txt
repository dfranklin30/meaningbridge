MeaningBridge Interactive AI Journal: Privacy, Sharing, and Crisis Safety

Concept Summary

MeaningBridge should include an interactive AI journal where users can write freely about grief, loss, guilt, anger, regret, love, trauma, or continuing bonds. Because some journal entries may feel too private to share immediately, each entry should have a clear privacy control that lets the user decide whether it is private or shared with their therapist/clinician.

The journal should create emotional safety by letting users express difficult feelings without feeling watched, while also including crisis safeguards for self-harm, suicide risk, abuse, or imminent danger.

The goal is to balance:

User privacy
Therapeutic trust
Clinical usefulness
Safety and liability protection
Responsible AI oversight

Core Feature

Each journal entry has a visibility setting:

Private

The entry is not visible to the therapist by default. The user can write freely without immediate disclosure.

Shared With Therapist

The entry is visible to the therapist/clinician dashboard.

Share Later

The entry remains private now, but the user can choose to share it later.

AI-Assisted Reflection

Regardless of sharing status, the user may optionally ask the AI for reflection, grounding, emotional labeling, or gentle prompts.

Important Safety Principle

Even if an entry is marked private, MeaningBridge should reserve the right to run automated safety screening for crisis indicators.

This must be disclosed clearly to the user.

Suggested language:
“Your private journal entries are not shown to your therapist unless you choose to share them. However, MeaningBridge may use automated safety screening to detect signs of imminent harm, suicide risk, abuse, or emergency danger. If serious risk is detected, the system may prompt you to seek immediate support or may alert your clinician according to the safety policy you agreed to.”

Why This Matters

If users believe every word automatically goes to their therapist, they may withhold the most honest material. Privacy helps people write more openly. But if a user writes something like a suicide note, a final goodbye, or a direct plan to harm themselves, the system needs a defined response protocol.

Interactive AI Journal Behavior

The journal should not be a passive text box only. It should feel like a supportive reflective companion.

Examples of AI feedback:

“You seem to be carrying a lot of guilt here. Would it help to write what you wish you could say?”
“You mentioned feeling alone. Is this similar to what you felt in your last entry?”
“This sounds heavier than usual. Would you like a grounding exercise before continuing?”
“You wrote that you don’t want to be here anymore. Are you in immediate danger right now?”

Crisis Detection

The system should scan entries for indicators such as:

Suicidal thoughts
Self-harm intent
Goodbye/final letter language
Plans, means, or timing
Harm to others
Abuse or exploitation
Medical emergency
Severe dissociation or inability to stay safe

Risk Levels

Level 0: No Risk

Normal journaling. No intervention.

Level 1: Emotional Distress

AI offers support, grounding, journaling prompts, or encouragement to share with therapist.

Level 2: Concerning Language

AI encourages user to share with therapist and offers a one-click “share this entry” option.

Level 3: Possible Crisis

AI asks direct safety questions:

“Are you thinking about hurting yourself?”
“Do you have a plan?”
“Are you in immediate danger?”

The system may recommend contacting therapist, crisis line, trusted person, or emergency services.

Level 4: Imminent Risk

If the user expresses clear intent, plan, means, or immediate danger, the system triggers the crisis protocol:

Show emergency message
Encourage calling 911 or 988
Encourage contacting a trusted person
Alert clinician if the user has opted into clinician monitoring
Log the safety event
Preserve the entry for clinical review according to policy

Clinician Alert Logic

Private entries should not be visible to clinicians unless:

The user shares the entry, or
The entry triggers a defined safety threshold, and
The user has consented to the crisis safety policy during onboarding.

Clinician alerts should include:

Risk level
Timestamp
Entry excerpt or full entry, depending on policy
Recommended response
Whether the user was prompted to contact emergency support

User Consent During Onboarding

MeaningBridge should clearly explain:

Private means private from the therapist unless shared.
AI safety screening may still occur.
Certain crisis indicators may override privacy.
The app is not an emergency service.
If the user is in immediate danger, they should call 911 or 988.
Clinician monitoring depends on the care model and consent settings.

Suggested Onboarding Consent Text

“MeaningBridge is a reflective grief-support tool, not an emergency service. You may keep journal entries private from your therapist, or choose to share them. To help protect your safety, MeaningBridge may use automated screening to detect signs of serious or imminent harm. If a serious safety concern is detected, the system may provide crisis resources and, where applicable, notify your clinician or care team according to your consent settings.”

Journal Entry UI Requirements

Each journal entry should include:

Title
Date/time
Mood check-in
Text entry area
Privacy toggle: Private / Shared / Share Later
AI reflection toggle
“Ask MeaningBridge to reflect” button
“Share with therapist” button
“Save as private” button
“I need help now” crisis support button

Therapist Dashboard Requirements

Therapists should be able to see:

Shared journal entries
Entries flagged for crisis risk
Risk level summaries
User mood trends
User-selected themes
Conversation history only where permission is granted
Safety events and timestamps

Therapists should not see private entries unless shared or safety protocol is triggered.

Product Positioning

This feature should be positioned as a private, emotionally safe journal with optional sharing and responsible crisis safeguards.

It should not feel like surveillance. It should feel like:
“You control what you share. We protect your privacy. And if the system detects that you may be in danger, MeaningBridge will help you get support.”
